1. Market Structure Framework

The Competitive Spectrum

Market structures exist on a continuum from perfect competition to monopoly. Monopolistic competition and oligopoly represent intermediate forms where strategic interaction becomes crucial. These markets are characterized by imperfect competition, where firms have some degree of market power but face competitive pressures.

Market Structure Number of Firms Product Differentiation Barriers to Entry Price Setting Power
Perfect Competition Many Homogeneous None None (Price Taker)
Monopolistic Competition Many Differentiated Low Limited
Oligopoly Few May be differentiated High Significant
Monopoly One Unique Very High Maximum

Key Insight: The transition from many to few firms fundamentally changes competitive dynamics. Strategic interdependence emerges when firms are large enough that their actions materially affect competitors' profits.

2. Monopolistic Competition Deep Dive

Structural Characteristics

  • Large Number of Firms: Typically dozens to hundreds of competitors
  • Product Differentiation: Real or perceived differences in products/services
  • Free Entry and Exit: Low barriers allow market entry and exit
  • Independent Decision Making: Firms largely ignore competitors' reactions
  • Local Market Power: Limited pricing power due to brand loyalty or location

Economic Behavior

In monopolistic competition, firms face downward-sloping demand curves due to product differentiation, giving them some pricing power. However, this power is constrained by:

  • Availability of close substitutes
  • Easy entry of new competitors
  • Consumer price sensitivity
  • Limited brand loyalty

Short-Run vs. Long-Run Equilibrium

Short Run: Firms can earn positive economic profits if they successfully differentiate their products and create customer loyalty.

Long Run: Economic profits attract new entrants, increasing competition and reducing individual firm market shares. The process continues until economic profits are eliminated.

Long-Run Equilibrium Condition:
P = ATC (Economic profits = 0)
P > MC (Monopolistic element persists)
Excess capacity exists (firms operate left of minimum ATC)

Examples of Monopolistic Competition

  • Restaurants: Cuisine types, ambiance, location differentiation
  • Retail Clothing: Style, brand image, quality differences
  • Hair Salons: Service quality, convenience, stylist expertise
  • Coffee Shops: Atmosphere, location, specialty drinks
  • Local Services: Plumbers, electricians, mechanics

3. Oligopoly Market Dynamics

Defining Features

  • Few Dominant Firms: Typically 2-10 major players
  • High Market Concentration: Top firms control large market shares
  • Significant Barriers to Entry:
    • High capital requirements
    • Economies of scale
    • Government regulations
    • Patent protection
    • Network effects
  • Strategic Interdependence: Each firm's decisions significantly impact competitors
  • Price Leadership: Often one firm sets prices, others follow

Types of Oligopoly

Homogeneous Oligopoly

Firms produce identical or nearly identical products (e.g., steel, cement, gasoline).

Differentiated Oligopoly

Firms produce differentiated products (e.g., automobiles, smartphones, airlines).

Strategic Considerations

Oligopolistic firms must consider:

  • Competitor Reactions: How will rivals respond to price changes?
  • Capacity Decisions: Investment timing and scale effects
  • Product Innovation: R&D competition and first-mover advantages
  • Market Entry: Deterrence strategies and competitive responses
  • Cooperative Behavior: Implicit coordination vs. explicit collusion

4. Game Theory Foundations

What is Game Theory?

Game theory is the mathematical study of strategic decision-making among rational agents. Developed by John von Neumann and Oskar Morgenstern, it provides a framework for analyzing situations where:

  • Multiple decision-makers interact
  • Each player's payoff depends on all players' choices
  • Players must anticipate others' actions when making decisions
  • Outcomes are determined by the combination of all choices

Core Elements of a Game

Players

The decision-makers in the game. In industrial organization:

  • Firms competing for market share
  • Governments setting policies
  • Consumers making purchase decisions

Strategies

Complete action plans specifying what a player will do in every possible situation. Examples:

  • Pure Strategies: Specific actions (set price at $10)
  • Mixed Strategies: Probability distributions over actions (price high 70% of time)

Payoffs

The outcomes players receive for each combination of strategies, typically measured as:

  • Profits for firms
  • Utility for consumers
  • Welfare for society

5. Nash Equilibrium & Strategic Solutions

Nash Equilibrium Definition

A Nash equilibrium is a strategy profile where each player's strategy is a best response to all other players' strategies. Formally:

Nash Equilibrium Condition:
For each player i and strategy profile s* = (s₁*, s₂*, ..., sₙ*):
uᵢ(sᵢ*, s₋ᵢ*) ≥ uᵢ(sᵢ, s₋ᵢ*) for all sᵢ ∈ Sᵢ

Where:
uᵢ = player i's payoff function
sᵢ* = player i's equilibrium strategy
s₋ᵢ* = other players' equilibrium strategies

The Prisoner's Dilemma

The most famous game in game theory, illustrating how individual rationality can lead to collectively suboptimal outcomes.

Cooperate Defect
Cooperate 3, 3 0, 5
Defect 5, 0 1, 1

The dilemma: Mutual defection (1,1) is the Nash equilibrium, but mutual cooperation (3,3) would be better for both players.

6. Classical Strategic Models

Cournot Competition Model

Developed by Antoine Augustin Cournot (1838), this model analyzes oligopoly where firms compete by choosing quantities simultaneously.

Key Assumptions

  • Firms choose quantities (not prices)
  • Homogeneous product
  • Market price determined by total supply
  • Firms have identical cost structures
  • Simultaneous decision-making
Cournot Equilibrium (Duopoly):
Market demand: P = a - b(q₁ + q₂)
Firm costs: C(qᵢ) = cqᵢ
Equilibrium quantities: q₁* = q₂* = (a-c)/(3b)
Equilibrium price: P* = (a+2c)/3
Firm profits: π* = [(a-c)/(3b)]² × b

Bertrand Competition Model

Named after Joseph Bertrand (1883), this model studies price competition between firms.

Bertrand Paradox

With homogeneous products and identical costs, price competition drives prices to marginal cost, eliminating economic profits even with only two firms.

Bertrand Equilibrium:
With identical marginal costs c:
P₁* = P₂* = c (marginal cost pricing)
π₁* = π₂* = 0 (zero economic profits)

7. Advanced Game Theory Concepts

Dominant Strategies

Strictly Dominant Strategy

A strategy that yields higher payoffs than any other strategy, regardless of opponents' choices.

Dominated Strategy Elimination

Rational players never choose dominated strategies. Iteratively eliminating dominated strategies can simplify games and predict outcomes.

Repeated Games

When games are played repeatedly, cooperation becomes more sustainable through:

  • Trigger Strategies: Cooperate until someone defects, then punish
  • Tit-for-Tat: Copy opponent's previous action
  • Reputation Effects: Long-term gains from trustworthy behavior

8. Market Power & Welfare Analysis

Sources of Market Power

In Monopolistic Competition

  • Product differentiation and brand loyalty
  • Location advantages
  • Customer switching costs
  • Information asymmetries

In Oligopoly

  • Economies of scale and scope
  • High capital requirements
  • Network effects
  • Patent protection
  • Government licensing
  • Strategic deterrence
Welfare Measures:
Consumer Surplus = ∫[from 0 to Q] P(q)dq - P×Q
Producer Surplus = P×Q - ∫[from 0 to Q] MC(q)dq
Deadweight Loss = CS(competitive) + PS(competitive) - CS(actual) - PS(actual)

9. Strategic Behavior & Collusion

Types of Collusion

Explicit Collusion

Direct communication and agreement between firms:

  • Formal cartels (often illegal)
  • Price-fixing agreements
  • Market division schemes
  • Output restrictions

Tacit Collusion

Coordination without explicit communication:

  • Price Leadership: One firm sets prices, others follow
  • Parallel Behavior: Similar responses to market conditions
  • Focal Points: Natural coordination points (round numbers, industry standards)
Collusion Sustainability Condition:
Present value of collusion ≥ Deviation profit + Present value of punishment
π(collude)/(1-δ) ≥ π(deviate) + δ×π(punishment)/(1-δ)
Where δ = discount factor

10. Product Differentiation Strategies

Types of Differentiation

Horizontal Differentiation

Products differ in characteristics valued differently by consumers:

  • Location (closer/farther)
  • Flavor/style preferences
  • Feature combinations
  • Brand personality

Vertical Differentiation

Products differ in quality levels:

  • High-end vs. budget versions
  • Premium materials/components
  • Service quality levels
  • Reliability differences

11. Dynamic Competition & Innovation

Innovation Competition

In knowledge-intensive industries, firms compete through:

  • Research and development investment
  • Patent races
  • Technology adoption timing
  • Platform development

Network Effects and Standards

Markets with network externalities exhibit special characteristics:

  • Winner-Take-All Dynamics: Strong first-mover advantages
  • Standard Wars: Competition for market standard
  • Switching Costs: Lock-in effects and market tipping
  • Platform Competition: Ecosystem vs. ecosystem rivalry

12. Regulatory Implications

Antitrust Policy

Merger Policy

Evaluating horizontal mergers:

  • Market concentration measures (HHI)
  • Efficiency benefits vs. market power costs
  • Entry barriers and potential competition
  • Innovation effects
Herfindahl-Hirschman Index (HHI):
HHI = Σᵢ sᵢ² × 10,000
Where sᵢ = market share of firm i

HHI < 1,500: Unconcentrated
1,500 ≤ HHI < 2,500: Moderately concentrated
HHI ≥ 2,500: Highly concentrated

14. Mathematical Foundations

Reaction Functions

In Cournot competition, firm i's reaction function shows optimal quantity given competitors' quantities:

Cournot Reaction Function:
Maximize: πᵢ = [P(Q) - cᵢ] × qᵢ
Where: Q = Σⱼ qⱼ (total quantity)
First-order condition: ∂πᵢ/∂qᵢ = 0
Reaction function: qᵢ = Rᵢ(q₋ᵢ)

Mixed Strategy Equilibrium

When no pure strategy Nash equilibrium exists, players randomize:

Mixed Strategy Condition:
Player must be indifferent between strategies in support
E[uᵢ(s₁, σ₋ᵢ)] = E[uᵢ(s₂, σ₋ᵢ)]
Where σ₋ᵢ = opponents' mixed strategies
