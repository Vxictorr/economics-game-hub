1. Definition and Basic Concepts

What is Price Elasticity of Demand?

Price elasticity of demand measures how responsive the quantity demanded of a good is to changes in its price. It tells us by what percentage quantity demanded will change when price changes by 1%.

Key Insight: Price elasticity helps businesses and policymakers understand how consumers will react to price changes, making it crucial for pricing strategies and market analysis.

Why is Elasticity Important?

  • Business Strategy: Helps companies set optimal prices
  • Revenue Prediction: Determines whether price increases will boost or reduce revenue
  • Market Understanding: Reveals consumer behavior patterns
  • Policy Making: Guides taxation and regulation decisions

2. Calculating Price Elasticity

Basic Formula

Price Elasticity of Demand (PED) = % Change in Quantity Demanded / % Change in Price

Or more precisely:
PED = (ΔQ/Q) / (ΔP/P)

Where:
• ΔQ = Change in quantity demanded
• Q = Original quantity
• ΔP = Change in price
• P = Original price

Step-by-Step Calculation

Example: Coffee Shop Price Increase

Scenario: A coffee shop increases price from $4 to $5, and daily sales drop from 100 to 80 cups.

Step 1: Calculate percentage change in price
% Change in Price = (($5 - $4) / $4) × 100 = 25%

Step 2: Calculate percentage change in quantity
% Change in Quantity = ((80 - 100) / 100) × 100 = -20%

Step 3: Calculate elasticity
PED = -20% / 25% = -0.8

Interpretation: Demand is inelastic (|PED| < 1)

The Negative Sign

Price elasticity is typically negative because of the law of demand (price up, quantity down). We often use the absolute value when classifying elasticity types.

3. Types of Price Elasticity

Classification by Elasticity Coefficient

Type Coefficient Range Description Examples
Perfectly Inelastic |PED| = 0 Quantity doesn't change with price Life-saving medicine
Inelastic 0 < |PED| < 1 Quantity changes less than price Gasoline, bread, utilities
Unitary Elastic |PED| = 1 Quantity changes proportionally to price Some clothing items
Elastic |PED| > 1 Quantity changes more than price Luxury cars, restaurant meals
Perfectly Elastic |PED| = ∞ Infinite response to price change Perfect substitutes

Visual Representation

  • Inelastic demand: Steep demand curve (vertical)
  • Elastic demand: Flat demand curve (horizontal)
  • Unitary elastic: Rectangular hyperbola

4. Factors Affecting Elasticity

1. Availability of Substitutes

  • Many substitutes: More elastic (easy to switch)
  • Few substitutes: Less elastic (harder to switch)
  • Example: Pepsi vs. Coca-Cola (elastic) vs. insulin (inelastic)

2. Necessity vs. Luxury

  • Necessities: Inelastic (must buy regardless of price)
  • Luxuries: Elastic (can postpone or avoid purchase)
  • Example: Bread (necessity) vs. jewelry (luxury)

3. Time Period

  • Short-run: More inelastic (less time to adjust)
  • Long-run: More elastic (more time to find alternatives)
  • Example: Gasoline demand more elastic over years than days

4. Proportion of Income

  • Small proportion: More inelastic (salt, matches)
  • Large proportion: More elastic (cars, houses)

5. Brand Loyalty

  • Strong brand loyalty: More inelastic
  • Weak brand loyalty: More elastic

6. Addictive Nature

  • Addictive products: Very inelastic (cigarettes, alcohol)
  • Non-addictive products: More elastic

5. Elasticity and Total Revenue

The Revenue Test

The relationship between elasticity and total revenue helps businesses make pricing decisions:

Total Revenue = Price × Quantity

Revenue Effects of Price Changes

Demand Type Price Increase Price Decrease Revenue Maximizing Strategy
Elastic
(|PED| > 1)		 Revenue decreases Revenue increases Lower prices
Unitary Elastic
(|PED| = 1)		 Revenue unchanged Revenue unchanged Any price works
Inelastic
(|PED| < 1)		 Revenue increases Revenue decreases Higher prices

Revenue Calculation Example

Elastic Product (Luxury watches, PED = -2.5):

  • Initial: $1,000 price, 100 units sold = $100,000 revenue
  • 20% price increase: $1,200 price, 50 units sold = $60,000 revenue
  • Result: Revenue decreased by 40%

Inelastic Product (Gasoline, PED = -0.3):

  • Initial: $3.00 price, 1,000 gallons = $3,000 revenue
  • 20% price increase: $3.60 price, 940 gallons = $3,384 revenue
  • Result: Revenue increased by 12.8%

6. Real-World Applications

Business Pricing Strategies

Technology Companies

  • Streaming services: Often use low prices due to elastic demand
  • Software subscriptions: Price discrimination based on elasticity
  • Smartphones: Premium pricing for inelastic brand-loyal customers

Retail and Consumer Goods

  • Luxury brands: High prices despite elastic demand (exclusivity)
  • Grocery stores: Low margins on inelastic necessities
  • Airlines: Dynamic pricing based on elasticity patterns

Government Policy Applications

Taxation

  • Sin taxes: High taxes on inelastic goods (cigarettes, alcohol)
  • Luxury taxes: May backfire on elastic luxury goods
  • Fuel taxes: Effective revenue source due to inelastic demand

Price Controls

  • Rent control: More effective on inelastic housing demand
  • Minimum wage: Effects depend on labor demand elasticity
  • Healthcare pricing: Inelastic demand allows regulation

Market Analysis

Competitive Strategy

  • Price wars: More damaging in elastic markets
  • Market entry: Easier in elastic markets with price competition
  • Brand differentiation: Reduces elasticity and price sensitivity
